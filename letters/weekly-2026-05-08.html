03

Featured: STRC’s Par Ceiling — Why We’re Trimming

An insight worth banking. STRC is engineered to trade at par. Strategy runs what’s called an at-the-market issuance facility on STRC: any time STRC trades meaningfully above $100, Saylor uses the ATM to issue more STRC shares and uses the proceeds to buy BTC. The new supply pushes STRC back toward par. When STRC trades below par, Strategy can step the variable dividend rate up or buy back to defend par. Either way, the price is bounded by design. STRC will not break above $100 in any sustained way because the issuer has explicit, programmatic incentive to dilute at any premium.

The implication is sharper than it sounds. Every share of STRC we own is effectively at or near its maximum capital appreciation right now. Our best-cost lots — the 11/20/25 share at $90.27 (post-ROC adjusted), the 12/11/25 lot at $94.82, the 2/6/26 lot at $94.84 — have already captured 99%+ of their possible price gain at today’s $99.95 spot. Holding longer doesn’t add meaningful upside; selling now harvests what’s structurally available.

This is not “selling winners” in the casual sense. It’s recognizing that one of our sleeves is a yielding instrument, not a growth instrument, and that its capital ceiling has been reached. The whole reason STRC is in the book is the 11.5% dividend — the cash it generates every month, the income engine of the fund. The price chart is incidental. Trimming a small slice of STRC at near-par and rotating proceeds into BTC (the uncapped growth asset) is exactly aligned with how the doctrine should treat these two instruments differently. STRC is for income. BTC is for growth. We sell STRC at the ceiling. We buy BTC on fear. We do not confuse the two.

Practically: we plan to sell 10 shares of STRC from our 2/6/26 lot ($94.84 cost basis) at market early next week, raising approximately $999 in IBKR cash and realizing a $51 capital gain that passes through to Mike’s K-1 (negligible at this scale). Combined with existing cash this brings the cash sleeve back to ~$1,400 — near target weight after running thin since Wednesday’s Swan deployment.

Why We’re Trimming STRC Next Week

  • Cash sleeve refill. After the May 6 BTC deployment, cash dropped to ~$413 (2% of NAV, well below the 5–10% target). The trim raises ~$999 to refill it without requiring fresh capital from Mike or a bank-to-IBKR transfer.
  • Capital appreciation on STRC is structurally exhausted (see featured section above). The 2/6 lot at $94.84 cost basis sitting at $99.95 spot has captured 99.7% of its possible price gain. The marginal benefit of holding rather than rotating is approximately 5 cents per share.
  • STRC sleeve is at-target weight already (37% vs ~35% target). Trimming 10 sh moves it to 32%, which is still in range. We are not under-allocating to the income engine; we are recognizing that one specific sleeve has reached its capital ceiling and the dollars redeploy more usefully elsewhere over time.

Why We’re Switching DRIP Off after May 30

STRC has been auto-reinvesting its monthly distributions into more STRC shares since inception. With the par-ceiling thesis ratified, that policy is no longer optimal: every additional STRC share we DRIP into has the same structurally-capped capital appreciation. Switching the DRIP setting off (one toggle in IBKR) routes future distributions into IBKR cash instead. As that cash accumulates — roughly $65 per month at the current 11.5% rate and current sleeve size — we’ll bundle it into Swan transfers every quarter or so to buy spot BTC. The income engine becomes a BTC accumulator instead of a STRC compounder. The yield doesn’t change. The destination of the yield does.

When in doubt, do less. Document before execute. The benchmark is BTC, not dollars. Size to the signal, not the headline. No leverage on spot BTC, ever.
