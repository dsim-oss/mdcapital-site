01

Macro Environment

The macro tape rewarded risk this week with very little new information. There were no headline policy events. The Iran–U.S. ceasefire held. The dollar drifted weaker against most majors. Spot Bitcoin ETFs printed approximately $996M of inflows last week — the strongest weekly intake since January — and the buying continued through this week’s tape. None of this is, on its own, a regime change. Together it produced the cleanest BTC week we’ve had since the early-February peak.

The interesting macro fact is the velocity of the sentiment unwind. Last Friday the Fear & Greed index sat at 23, deep in extreme fear. By Tuesday it printed 54. Wednesday: 46. Friday: 60, in greed territory. We have gone from a market that refused to price the rally to a market that is starting to lean into it — in five business days, with no obvious macro catalyst beyond a calendar that did not surprise to the downside. That is the tell of positioning, not fundamentals: a thinly-positioned market gets long quickly when the headline tape stops fighting it.

The implication for the doctrine is straightforward. Our accumulation triggers are contrarian by design. They fire when fear is extreme, not when the rally is already in progress. Last week we had two of three signals firing and chose not to chase strength; this week the BTC accumulation signal is clearly quiet, the MSTR discount-to-NAV signal has effectively closed, and only STRC sub-par is still live. The framework is doing what it’s supposed to do: it does not generate trades when the setup is not there.

02

Bitcoin This Week

Spot BTC traded a range of approximately $75,178 to $78,317, closing the week at the upper end. Friday’s close at $78,317 is the highest print since early February and breaks roughly two months of range-trading between $70k and $76k. The Fear & Greed index moved from 23 last Friday to 60 this Friday, a 37-point swing in a single week. We have not seen a sentiment move of that magnitude in either direction since the November 2025 cycle peak.

The composition of the bid is what we are watching. Spot ETFs continued to take in capital steadily — roughly $240M on a single session midweek — and the trailing 30-day flows are no longer tepid. Strategy’s own buying program (see Section 03) accounted for a meaningful share of the spot demand on its disclosed purchase dates. There is no obvious sign of late-cycle retail froth in the data we watch — perp funding rates are positive but not extreme, and the on-chain HODL waves still show coins moving from short-term to long-term holders. That is constructive for the medium term and consistent with the 2024-style setup, even if it does not, on its own, justify a chase here.

Our BTC accumulation signal (Fear & Greed ≤ 20) did not fire this week and is unlikely to fire again without either a fresh sub-$70k retest or a renewed external shock. That is fine. The doctrine is patient by construction. We will wait.

03

Featured: Strategy Passes BlackRock

The institutional development of the week, by a wide margin, was Strategy’s Monday disclosure of its third-largest BTC purchase in company history. Between approximately April 14 and April 20, Strategy added 34,164 BTC at an average price of $74,395, deploying $2.54 billion in a single weekly window. Total Strategy holdings now sit at 815,061 BTC, or roughly 3.8% of the eventual 21 million-coin supply, at a cumulative cost basis of approximately $60.5 billion.

As of Monday’s disclosure, Strategy now holds more spot BTC than BlackRock’s IBIT ETF — the largest institutional Bitcoin allocator on the planet by AUM — and the gap is widening. This is a milestone we did not expect to see crossed this early in the cycle. It rearranges the institutional landscape: the largest single-entity BTC holder is now an operating company, not a passive ETF wrapper, with an active capital-raising flywheel funding incremental buys every week.

The funding mix on this purchase is the cleanest illustration we’ve had of that flywheel. Of the $2.54B deployed, approximately $2.18B (86%) came through STRC preferred-stock at-the-market issuance, with the remaining $366M (14%) through MSTR common ATM. The preferred-stack is doing the heavy lifting and the common-stack dilution is being kept proportionally small. That is the right rotation for our positioning: we own STRC at the funding side of the trade and MSTR on the buy-the-NAV-discount side, and the company’s preference for preferred-stock issuance protects the per-share NAV math we run for the MSTR signal.

TD Cowen raised their MSTR price target to $385 on the disclosure, citing both the BTC accumulation pace and the demonstrated ATM execution capability. We don’t take street price targets as a buy-or-sell input — the doctrine uses NAV-discount as the trigger, not consensus — but the target is a proxy for how the market is reading the same underlying mechanics we are.

Why We Did Not Trade

Three reasons, in priority order. None of them are a vibe call.

  • The MSTR window closed faster than the cash arrived. The discount-to-NAV signal that fired through last Friday at −19.4% had compressed to roughly −7% by Wednesday and effectively closed by Friday. With $87 of IBKR cash on hand and no fresh transfer wired in from the operating account in time, we were mechanically constrained to one or zero shares of MSTR even on the days the discount was widest. We chose zero rather than commit fresh capital on a closing window. The bank-to-IBKR transfer mechanic is now the most important unresolved process item on the desk.
  • The BTC accumulation signal did not fire. F&G held above 20 every session this week. The doctrine does not deploy on rallies; it deploys on fear. We are not in the business of paying up.
  • The STRC sub-par window narrowed even as it stayed open. STRC traded between $99.21 and $99.60 every session this week — the signal was technically live throughout — but the discount to par is now $0.40, not the $0.79 we had earlier in the month, and the dividend-record date is six business days away. With $87 of IBKR buying power, an STRC add of meaningful size would have required the same bank transfer the MSTR add would have. We chose to wait until that mechanic is solved rather than rush a small fill at the narrowest point of the sub-par window.
When in doubt, do less. The default move is no trade. We size to the signal, not to the headline. The benchmark is BTC, not dollars, not the S&P. No leverage on spot BTC, ever.

Risks We Are Watching

  • Sentiment whiplash. A 37-point F&G swing in five sessions cuts both ways. Markets that get long this fast can also reverse fast. We are not short of upside if the trend continues, but the next leg lower — if there is one — is the leg the doctrine is built to deploy into. We hold cash in the bank specifically for this scenario.
  • Composition risk vs. the BTC scoreboard. Our BTC-denominated NAV slipped from 0.2311 BTC to 0.2279 BTC week-over-week despite the fund making money in dollars. That is the mathematical signature of carrying STRC and bank-cash weight while BTC outperforms. We accept this as the cost of the income leg, but it is the single most-likely path by which we lag the benchmark in a sustained BTC rally. Worth flagging cleanly: the fund will under-perform spot BTC in fast BTC up-moves and out-perform it in BTC drawdowns, by design.
  • STRC dilution velocity. Strategy raised $2.18B through STRC ATM in a single week. That is constructive for STRC holders — deeper liquidity, institutional buyers, dividend coverage well-supported — but it also means the preferred float is growing fast. If the issuance pace materially outruns Strategy’s ability to redeploy into BTC, the dividend coverage math could eventually thin. We are not at that level of concern today; we are tracking it.
  • MSTR discount may not re-open. The compression from −19.4% to roughly −7% in five sessions is the kind of NAV-gap close that often persists. The MSTR sleeve at 3.271 shares remains our primary directional exposure to any future re-rating, but we are no longer pricing-in another bite at the −15%-or-deeper discount in the near term. If the gap does re-open on a BTC pullback, the bank-to-IBKR transfer being executed early next week is what enables us to act on it.

A quiet week on the desk is not a quiet week on the work. We watched the discount we wanted close before we could act on it, sat through a sentiment move that reset the entire setup, and accepted that the BTC scoreboard tightened slightly against us as a fair price for the income leg the portfolio is built around. The doctrine held. The cash held. The next signal will get the deployment it deserves once the brokerage transfer is in place. More to come.
