Methodology Note — Time-Weighted Opportunity Cost

Prior letters reported the BTC scoreboard against a static “0.2000 BTC at $100,040” baseline derived by dividing the full $20,000 contribution by Swan’s lifetime average BTC basis. That math treats every dollar — including dollars actually deployed into MSTR and STRC at IBKR — as if it could have been bought at Swan’s blended average. It cannot. The MSTR and STRC dollars were deployed on specific trade dates when BTC was at specific spot prices, mostly during the February 2026 crash and the March bottom when BTC was at $66,000 to $72,000. The right comparison is to what BTC could have been bought on those same dates instead.

We rebuilt the calculation against the IBKR Activity Statement, fill by fill. The summary table is below; full detail (date, asset, qty, fill price, BTC@date, BTC alternative for each of 21 IBKR fills) is in the fund’s internal records.

The corrected scoreboard reads 0.2247 BTC actual vs. 0.2275 BTC alternative — −1.2% in BTC. The intuition is the inverse of last week’s framing. Yes, roughly 55% of capital was deployed at Swan in October–November 2025 when BTC was $107,000–$111,000, and BTC has drawn down to $80,895 since — but the IBKR sleeve was built almost entirely during the Q1 2026 BTC weakness and the early-April recovery, with weighted-average BTC at the IBKR fill dates of approximately $74,500. Buying STRC for $6,566 across that window meant we did not buy approximately 0.088 BTC that we could have. STRC’s flat-to-par price trajectory has not made up the gap; the MSTR sleeve’s rally has only partly offset it. The fund is behind opportunity cost. Honest answer.

Macro Environment

This was the inverse of the prior week’s tape. Two weeks ago Bitcoin broke $78,000 and sentiment ran from extreme fear to greed in five sessions on no fresh news. This week the news did not show up either, but the direction reversed: BTC printed a new local high at $81,000-plus and the Fear & Greed index walked back down from a 71 handle into the high-40s. Our 7-day sentiment delta is roughly negative 23 points, putting the index back in the “fear” band even as price made a higher high. That is an unusual print.

The cleanest read of the divergence is positioning: the rally that ran through the prior two weeks was thinly held, the new buyers were fast money, and the move through $80k flushed out conviction rather than building it. ETF flows confirmed the picture — the iShares Bitcoin ETF saw a $352M outflow session midweek, the largest single-day reversal since February. We are not in a regime change. We are in a tape that is still trying to figure out whether $80k is a ceiling or a floor.

Two scheduled events will resolve a meaningful share of that ambiguity: the FOMC rate decision on May 6–7 and the April CPI release on May 13. Neither has a policy surprise priced in; either one, if it surprises in either direction, repricies the BTC and equity risk premia. The doctrine’s response is unchanged. We do not pre-position for binary events. We watch the print, and if the tape obliges with a sub-30 F&G read or a sub-$70k retest, the framework deploys.

Featured: STRC Holds 11.5% — The First Real Stress Test

The most-important institutional development of the week, in our book, is not a Strategy 8-K. It is a non-event: Strategy elected to keep the STRC variable rate at 11.5% for the May payment cycle, the third consecutive month at that level. The reason this matters is mechanical. STRC’s rate-setting mechanism is calibrated against the prior month’s volume-weighted average price; April’s VWAP was $99.76, sitting just under par. The instrument has now closed below $100 every session since April 15 — nineteen consecutive trading days through today. Despite that persistent sub-par print, the issuer chose not to step the rate down.

This is the first real-world stress test of the STRC pricing mechanism since inception. The signal we read is straightforward: Strategy is treating the preferred-stock dividend coverage as a strategic priority and is willing to hold the rate constant rather than telegraph weakness on the highest-yielding capital tier in the stack. For our position, that means the 11.5% annualized yield we wrote into the original thesis is intact, the STRC sub-par window remains a credible accumulation trigger, and the income leg of the portfolio is doing exactly what it was designed to do on the income side — even as the corrected OC math (above) shows the same income leg cost us BTC accumulation in the rally.

In the same week, Ondo Finance announced a tokenized version of STRC on its rails, advertising the 11.5% monthly yield to on-chain treasuries and DeFi pools. We do not own tokenized STRC and have no current plan to. The relevance is that it widens the natural buyer base for the underlying instrument, which is constructive for the par-defense mechanic over time. Strategy now has a preferred-stock product with three distribution channels — traditional brokerage, IBKR-style retail, and tokenized DeFi — against a single dividend obligation. That is a structural tailwind for STRC liquidity and, by extension, for the integrity of our 11.5% yield assumption.

And this morning, before the cash open, Strategy reports Q1 earnings. Saylor confirmed last week that the firm paused weekly BTC purchases into the print, an unusual operational move and a small data point that the disclosure cadence may step into a different regime post-earnings. We will read the print cold and update the MSTR-NAV math against any fresh diluted-share count.

Why We Did Not Trade

Two reasons, both familiar from last week’s letter, neither resolved.

  • Bank-to-IBKR transfer is still pending. We flagged this as the operational priority for the week of April 27 and it did not happen. With $91 of IBKR buying power on hand, the MSTR discount briefly opened to −14.4% on April 30 — one point shy of trigger — and we were positioned to capture exactly zero of it. With $1,875 in the operating account and only $91 in IBKR, the realistic transfer this week is approximately $500–$750, leaving the bulk of the bank reserve intact for a Swan-side BTC tranche if a sub-$70k retest materializes.
  • The STRC sub-par window stayed thin. STRC traded between $99.21 and $99.95 every session. The signal was technically live throughout, but the discount to par sat at $0.05 to $0.79 — not a price worth chasing with the only buying power on hand, when an MSTR discount window might re-open and you’d want the dry powder for that. The corrected OC math (above) reinforces this: STRC purchases above $99 add modest BTC accumulation in the alt scenario; we don’t need to chase narrow sub-par windows.
When in doubt, do less. Document before execute. The benchmark is BTC, not dollars. Size to the signal, not the headline. No leverage on spot BTC, ever.

Risks We Are Watching

  • Operational drag is now a recurring risk. The bank-to-IBKR transfer being unresolved into a third consecutive week means the desk has been mechanically constrained from acting on two discrete signal windows. Process risk, not market risk. Process risks compound. The fix is operational, not analytical.
  • The fund is now 1.2% behind opportunity cost in BTC. With the corrected time-weighted methodology, we have crossed below the OC line. The gap is small and explainable — we built the income leg during the BTC weakness, exactly when buying spot BTC instead would have accumulated more coin. The gap is also narrowable: a BTC pullback materially closes it (because the cash and STRC sleeves don’t draw down with spot), and if we use the bank-to-IBKR transfer to add MSTR or STRC at a deeper discount, those incremental dollars could deploy into BTC instead via a Swan tranche on a sub-$70k retest. The choice of which way to lean is the open strategic question for next week.
  • BTC up-move risk. If BTC continues to grind higher without a pullback, our OC underperformance widens. Each $5,000 BTC higher costs us roughly 0.1 percentage points more on the OC scoreboard, holding the cash and yield sleeves constant. We are in a regime where the income leg is taxing the BTC scoreboard rather than supporting it. This is the design tradeoff acting in the unfavorable direction.
  • STRC rate-set risk shifts forward. The May rate-hold at 11.5% is constructive. The June rate-set will be calibrated against May’s VWAP, currently shaping up sub-par on the same persistent below-$100 print pattern. If Strategy steps the rate down to defend par, our income leg yield assumption revises lower — and the OC math gets worse, since the income offset to BTC opportunity-cost shrinks.
  • Q1 earnings reset risk. Saylor pausing the weekly purchase cadence ahead of the print is itself information. If the call surfaces a guidance change, the MSTR sleeve repricies first and the STRC sleeve second. The position size of the MSTR leg (3.271 sh, 3.3% of NAV) caps the exposure.
